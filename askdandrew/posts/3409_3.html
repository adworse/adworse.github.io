  • Bill
    Here is the risk with seconds that are in a state of NPN. They are obviously sitting behind a large first. If the payor stiffed the second lien holder, they have stiffed the first lien holder.

    Once a foreclosure action takes place and it will, it's only a matter of time, the second lien holder often gets wiped out because the second lien is junior to the senior debt in a enforceable action.

    The payments are most often very small so there is no real incentive to own these. Once legal is filed, there is very little that can be done to collect on these. The reason these are usually small value is because they were created in a structured deal where there was perhaps a 70% first! a 15% second and the buyer came to the table with 15% hard equity for the down.

    Since Sal reconditioned my thinking, my firm no longer trades in these and will not entertain submissions on these. That's my two cents.

  • staff

    David:

    Excellent question. We will fund the purchase of REOs. It is relatively easy to get a credit facility to do that. So if they fail, we foreclose on the collateral. This is Real Estate 101.

    However, to by NPNs, we *joint venture* on these. Why? Because they are more expensive to remedy via foreclosures, etc., so making sure they get in at a good *level* (or "basis") is critical. The holding costs coupled with the time to foreclose if not done correctly has buried many a novice.

    Many times I see someone who isn't an institutional pro *overbid* on these assets. Not by much, but by a margin that may put them closer to the redline.

    *Expert Tip*: One of our Intermediaries made a $2,000 consulting fee (too freaking low but she learned that lesson thankfully later after I spanked her - virtually - and gave her the self confidence and a script/pitch she needed to improve her self-esteem the next time) for using Dave Gordon’s Pricing Model on to price out this pool for someone as a second set of eyes or what we call in the industry a *”Fairness Opinion”*.

I am looking to see if they are not paying their taxes. This is a good barometer of whether there is financial distress on a property owner.

Now I go back for up to one year on each property and I'm looking to see if any legal has been filed and from this I can tell if a lender has done a workout, I can tell if the lender has had troubles off and on with this borrower.
