James Balter commented May 22 2014
  • staff

    James:

    Catch-ups are more prevalent in the opportunity fund (value added) sector. This is a defined split of profits that go to the investor and fund sponsor. A high catch-up split would be 80/20 that is 80 percent to the fund sponsor and 20 percent to the investor until such time as the sponsor has received its 20 percent return on overall profits. Even though the catch-up mechanism
    would normally kick in at the preferred return – for example, 9 percent – it would mean that for every dollar of profit over and above the 9 percent return, the fund sponsor would get 80 cents until the sponsor has received its 20 percent return on overall profit.

    Now this has changed a bit since 2008, but as is everything with these types of opportunistic funds, everything is negotiable.

I wanted to pass on something to the members that you have went out of the way to reinforce in getting my thinking to come full circle on the subject of risk.

Here goes. It's so easy to want a deal to be real, to put the cart before the horse, I do it, it's something that I'm working on. Sal has told me just because something looks like a deal, doesn't make it so.

Pragmatically speaking, a deal really isn't a deal until the deal is scrutinized, has gone through due diligence and terms are created and agreed to and then you can say it's a deal. Until that time, it's an opportunity that needs to prove out.

You have to look at the risks. This is prudent business. It's so easy to look at the financial rewards first because you want to get paid.

Everyone wants the big score, the huge fees, etc. This is why the players like Blackstone for example focus on the larger deals. Why they undertake capital raises to create a two billion dollar fund. They are building a financial war chest so they can buy those $250 million dollar deals and up, or the myriad of ninety million dollar resort deals, etc.

It's easy to bypass risk because as an Intermediary, you have no risk in a transaction because your not writing the check. Your risk is the time your investing at the front end and the only thing you risk is losing a payday and maybe a equity piece on the back end. The funder risks losing their capital, their credibility, etc. People never remember the great deals you do, they remember the bad deals.

You have to ask yourself what can possibly go wrong if my capital provider gets into this deal and it goes south at any point in time during the term of the deal. On a funding deal, what can go wrong at 12 months, 18 months, 24 months, the day before the term ends. On a syndicated deal, what happens if the sponsor pulls the trigger on a bad decision, are the investors going to have to do a capital call and put more equity in, just to make the deal whole, etc.

For a fund such as Dandrew, it's more than just capital. It's reputations, credibility, future abilities to gain the trust of investors to place monies again, investors monies, pension funds monies. This is why Sal and Dandrew just do not buy every deal. It's more than just deploying capital. Money has two voices and two tolerance levels, pleasure and pain.

When life in the deal is great, money is happy, it's growing, it's generating rates of return, it's generating cash on cash returns, etc. When life in a deal is not so great, money voices it's displeasure with those who had their hands on it. It's like a woman who is scorned and it gets even in ways that no one wants to be privy to.

Sal tells us all, just locate the deal, qualify it through the five data points, put together the package, and send it to him. The only one who can speak for Dandrew is Sal and AJ. Let them model the opportunity and make the determination if the risk is worth them buying the deal.

Always treat Dandrew's money as if it were yours. Be Sals Pal and look at risk first, reward second because at the end of the day, if the risks can not be properly evaluated and risk strategies arrived at and implemented, then there will be no rewards for anyone.

James Balter commented May 23 2014
  • staff

    James

    Very good question again.

    *1. Performing Notes:*
    *Yield* is what investors want. So this is why they bid down as compared to the face value of the note. The lower the bid, the higher the yield. *Basis* is not so much of an issue with *performing* notes.

    *2. Non-Performing Notes:*
    Basis. The lower the better. No yield so you’re buying this for control of the asset, not so much yield. And this is a function of costs and time to foreclose.

    *3. REOs*
    *Basis.* Some folks will tell you that they will pool and say it throws off a cap rate or NOI, but most *sophisticated (aka institutional) investors* will look at the basis only. If you’re talking to *retail investors* they are less sophisticated and will look at the rental cash flow, which in my opinion isn’t an accurate way to look at this product.

    Keep up the good work.
