James Balter commented Apr 21 2014
  • staff

    Simple.

    Private sellers aren’t as liquid and thus we have to provide what is called a *holdback* (as discussed in your training module) that will allow most of the proceeds to be released to the private seller, *but a portion that will be held back post-closing* for a pre-determined amount of time or until all agreed upon actions have been satisfactorily completed.

    This could mean anything from getting rid of squatters to making sure that the first month’s payments have *all* arrived to the buyer’s offices or servicing company of the buyer’s choosing.

    Or that all *Transfers and Assignments* have been recorded properly, so the buyer is now firmly in place on the chain of title.

    It’s a “carrot and stick” incentive structure for Bulk REO and NPL trades.

Ralph Stanyard commented Apr 20 2014
  • staff

    Ralph.

    You're assuming that a borrower is cooperative 100% of the time. The #1 reason why a bank or lender would sell a note is because they believe that:

    a) the borrower is not solvent, needs to be replaced.

    b) the borrower is not experienced, ran the project to the ground. Probably doesn't want to relinquish control. New note owner will foreclose.

    c) bank or lender is afraid that the borrower many file BK. If this is the case, the lender could be thrown over a barrel. Not good.

    d) bank or lender wants a quick closing and feels as though it is faster - and will discount accordingly - the note for a fast and efficient sale to a strong buyer. No need to play carrot and stick with the current borrower.

    e) Probate issues as borrower / operator has died (happens more than you think)

I wanted to pass on something you said to me when we did a call a few months back about a fund idea. I took note of everything you told me, but this stood out.

I think those who are new need to understand something. You must know your deal and you must be passionate because you believe in the deal.

There are deals located that you just know will never fly and you don't get passionate about and then there are deals that will surface that you just know are worth doing.

A guy on the call today was not prepared to talk about a deal. I think we all saw how that went down. Sal and AJ were very nice when others would not have been.

All the spreadsheet templates, the five data points and other tools you have at your disposal are for the purpose of crafting the presentation to investors and funders for your deal. It all starts with the five data points and evolves from there.

The five data points gives you a birds eye view of the deal. What is the significance of the current NOI? well the debt service payments come out of this and it shows where the properties financials are. What is the stabilized or Pro Forma? This is going to show where the project can be financially over time.

What's the debt stack? Will adding new capital cause the property to be over leveraged? In other words, will new capital put the property in danger of not making debt service. Sources and uses, the story behind the deal, exit strategy.

You must know this, it's a huge part of knowing the deal. One thing you also have to understand is what is the best deal structure? meaning, does the project lend itself to a refi? will bringing in a JV partner be more appropriate?

The guy on the call was talking about a multi family in Fort Worth, Texas, 186 units, 80% occupancy and he mentioned Loop Net, also known as hospice for deals. Just because it's 186 units and 80% occupied does not mean anyone will jump at this deal.

Most investors will compare a deal like this to others in the market. They will look at what's available for sale, what is the class of the asset, meaning is it A, B or C. The biggest question will always be, do I spend $2,000,000.00 for 186 units at 80% occupancy and a class C property that needs rehab, new management, new signage, extensive advertising and marketing to get occupancy raised or do I invest $2,850,000.00 for 240 units in a class A property where it's pretty much turn key.

You must ask yourself, if I had the capital available whether it's through a combination of my own or a partners equity and a new first or whatever the capital stack is, would I buy this deal? you have to look past dollars and cents because pragmatically, the deal just may not be worth doing.

Richard Belliveau commented Apr 8 2014

Sal, in reading some of the posts here, I've come to see that a lot if the members are new and trying bulk deals. I applaud everyone for being here and working to make it big.

I wanted to pass this on as a few tricks I use to determine if I'm dealing with a untrained Intermediary and determining if they are as direct as they want me to believe.

First question I ask is, so your direct to the seller? Usual answer is yes.

Next question is, who is the seller? Usual answer is can't provide that right now and my question us, why not

My statement than becomes, okay, well are they an individual, family office looking to divest, bank, usual answer is cannot provide that right now.

Next question is, when can you? Usual answer is always something like, when you submit a LOI and POF.

Next question is? Letter of interest or Letter of Intent. There is a difference. Letter of Interest just says you are interested and under what terms you usually buy at, it's very vanilla in nature. Letter of intent spells out what terms you'll buy at and what price you would buy at if everything meets your needs.

Next statement is, if your direct to the seller than you have a agreement for compensation in place and usually they say yes.

My next statement than becomes, well if this is the case than even if I went around you direct, you would be protected anyway no matter what, because if your direct and your the only one who has this deal, than the seller, who you have an agreement with knows I got the deal direct from you.

At this point, I usually have outed them and their only thing to cling to is a POF and I say well since we have already concluded you are not direct than I'm not sending you a POF, I will send it to the seller at the proper stage, where do I send it to and usually this is when they confirm they are not direct because they can't tell you who the seller is, they only know who they got the deal from.

My next question is how many points are you getting in the deal and usually they say something like its 50 or 40 plus 3. I then call them out again by saying ok, what are you doing that's worth three points? Usually I get any myriad of replies.

I lead the call, I ask pointed questions, I listen to the replies, I listen to the tones in the other persons voice, I listen for tells, which is what professional poker players do when they are sizing up their co-players.

From the call I can tell if I am dealing with a novice or a seasoned player, a Intermediary or even a direct seller. Yes, sometimes you will get a seller who is great as a buyer but terrible as a seller.

You have to qualify the deal and you have to begin by qualifying the parties in the deal. You must know who your working with. Sorry this is long winded.
