In the context of "Funding Sources," I like to think of "strategic investors" as individuals or entities that align their capital with specific outcomes. If their desired outcome is proliferation of green energy, they may be inclined to invest in projects that include a green energy component, for example. If their desired outcome is gentrification of a given sub-market, their investment may be geographically focused - and only provided in the presence of a business plan that furthers gentrification.

While I don't think of "strategic investors" as having a specific place in a capital stack, I suppose if the desired outcome is risk averse consistent returns (as may be found in some pension plans and insurance companies), they may be inclined to limit their investment to a defined waterline in the debt stack - say 65% of the value and only provided as a debt instrument. They may never venture into the equity portion under any circumstance. The reverse could also be true where an investor has a allocation specifically for higher risk / higher return. This investor may target common equity or an aggressive preferred equity position.

A more Dandrew specific example may be an investor who is looking for a bond alternative and opts to set aside an allocation solely for grocery anchored retail in the mid-west. Their strategic outcome may not be to support grocery stores, but to shelter their capital from volatility.

The important take away, from my perspective, relative to "strategic investors," is that for better or worse, they are more polarized than their counterparts. They are looking for specificity in an investment that aligns with their desired outcome. If we can deliver that specificity in a project - or on the flip side, if we can find the investor who's strategy is aligned with a project - we are more likely to close a deal.

From a marketing perspective it becomes important, therefore, to understand how strategic investors group and move. For example, ESG investors (those who align their investment capital with environmental, social and governance issues) might be found paying attention to business news that highlights advancement in green energy. If we want to serve them, we get ourselves in front of a group of them with our message - maybe by placing an ad in a magazine that caters to them, by giving a talk at a local chamber of commerce, or by following affiliate organizations that focus their efforts in that direction.

    Harold,

    I wouldn’t mix the two terms “syndication” and “angel” in the same sentence outside of this forum.

    Here’s why: *“syndication”* or *“tenants-in-common”* refer to people investing in a real estate deal. There is little perceived risk and there is upside, but for the most part, they don’t have to worry about a lot of risk to get a return or be somewhat secured in their investment.

    *“Angel”* investment refers to some investing in a brand new, fresh-out-of-the-box startup, with no proven track record. This is the “Shark Tank” BS people love to watch. Higher risk, greater rewards.

    Comparatively speaking, an apartment complex is a proven revenue model, that is why banks lend on them.

    Does this make sense, Harold? Its important – at least to me – that *you* understand the distinction because I don’t know who you’ll be talking to and sometimes you only get 1 chance to make a good, solid impression.

    Your pal,
    Sal
