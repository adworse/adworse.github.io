    Nique

    First, that was a nice check. *$37,000 is not a bad payday.* Now you have the deal junkie virus in your blood. You’ll never recover!

    Great question. If you’re doing residential deals, you’re better off doing them on a deal-by-deal basis. Now, before you take me off of the Christmas card list, listen to what I have to tell you:

    *1. You’ll make more money – in the short term – by taking 5 points or whatever you’re charging.*
    Your investors will want to see you more in action. Give them checks. But more than that, make sure that everything you’re doing is buttoned down – meaning, place them into a servicing company who sends them automatic payments or checks. It is most important for you to show them how you are “managing” their money. Not just collecting a fee. Make sense? You’re building a brand here for yourself.

    *2. If, and only if, you can find enough deal flow, do a small raise around that.*
    This is where people get into trouble. They take in capital, but they feel pressured to deploy it. The worst reason to invest in anything or deploy capital in this case is because you feel like you have to.

    You can run a successful brokerage model without having to take money and lock it up.

    I think the reasons your investors are pushing back is that 2 years is a long time when you a) have a presidential election coming up, b) they want to be able to have discretion on what deals they go into and not rely solely on your discretion yet and c) the world is nervous as a result of Paris, etc.

    If you were to ask them after the next Presidential Election, then you may have a better chance of getting them to lock up with you. However, now, its going to be tough. Liquidity is key. And you must show that you have deal flow (see #2 above) to deploy it - *meaningfully*.

    Your Pal,
    Sal

  • Good morning, Nique. Hope you don't mind my chiming in. You were asking about how to show up on the HUD1, which would indicate to me you are not a licensed real estate broker, mortgage broker or securities broker/dealer, am I correct? All 50 states have enacted statues requiring anybody who sells real estate for another person or entity be licensed in that state. Also, since 2008 several states (13 to my knowledge) have enacted new statutes limiting who can broker commercial loans. Each state varies somewhat, but each of these states require some form of licensing. If my assumption that you are not some form of broker is correct, this is NOT bad news, it's good news!! Offer to take the title company underwriter out to lunch and explain to him/her your situation and what you want to do. The underwriter is usually an attorney and would be intimately knowledgeable regarding state license and lending laws. He/she may have other solutions to offer, but being legally compliant is simple. See if the underwriter knows of a broker who does similar investment deals and ask to be introduced to that broker. Set yourself up to be the investor, not the broker. Let your new "partner" be the broker, you then factor all of your fees into the deal as lender only . If all goes well, the broker and the title company underwriter will soon be coming to you to fund deals. You grow and everybody wins!! Keep in mind, most states have "implied" fiduciary obligations for real estate brokers, and if anything goes wrong with this deal (the buyer is a new investor getting her feet wet), well let's just say there are hefty fines and prison times that may be awaiting an unlicensed "broker". You don't need that! Never wear two hats in the same deal for your own protection. Hope this helps! Happy Investing!!

    Nique.
