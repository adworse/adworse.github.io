    Dexter:

    No. Hard money lenders are not affiliated with banks. But sometimes hard money lenders get what’s called “*credit facilities*” from other banks. This is adding leverage to what they have.

    For example, if a hard money lender has $5mm raised in investor equity, he can get a loan for an additional $5mm (or in good times $10mm).
    So now for $5mm, he’s making points and fees and interest income on $10mm. What a wonderful country, right?

    Except leverage can destroy you if you don’t know what you’re doing. And I’m here to tell you, Dexter, that many hard money lenders screw up operationally, and then blow up.

    So, then if you’re thinking like your pal Sal, you go to the bank, offer $4mm for the defaulted loan secured against the $10mm portfolio, and foreclose and get $10mm worth of assets for…. You guessed it! $4mm. So we would add $6mm to our net worth because we leverage our brains.

    Now, that’s what you call Grave Dancing.

    Sal

  • Dexter, years ago when I started in the business I was brokering notes. Back then the in thing was trading individual notes. Pools were for the big boys, I.e selling entity to the bigger Wall Street type buyers with deep pockets who understood the matrix on pools.

    I often worked with builders, mainly the smaller guys who did not always have the liquidity needed to secure development financing. Liquidity is a banking term that means having cash reserves on hand.

    How I structured deals was as follows. I setup a relationship with note buyers ahead of time. This was easy because I was in their eyes a shop that would always have product available. I was going to bring about opportunities for them to make money, I wasn't a once in awhile deal source.

    In the loan pool business and note business today, this is referred to as flow basis. This is what everyone wants. Once and dones are great but flow is where it is at.

    Builders were easy to get on board because what I was doing was increasing their market because they would simply advertise, "We offer Builder financing". This gave the builder a whole new market niche, because not many were offering this and it bought people in on Saturdays on a regular basis.

    The deals were structured as follows. 65/20/15. This meant Builder would create a first lien mortgage for 65%, a second mortgage for 20% and buyer had to come to the table with 15% down.

    The note buyer would run credit, qualify the deal and say yes or no. If it was a go, the title agent would create the docs and setup and administer the closing. The actual down was 10% but we added back in the anticipated 5% discount the builder would have had to take.

    I generated a commission of $500.00 per closed deal. When your a twenty something kid and your making $500.00 a deal, it's huge. If I did 5 per month, that was $2500.00 and back then that's like making $10,000 per month today.

    Now the note buyer would buy the first, the builder would hold the second for income. The note servicer would collect both payments and simply mail back to the builder his payment, minus fees.

    Now why would a buyer do this deal? the reason is that they couldn't buy a new home any other way and the two payments were not much more than what they would pay a bank anyway.
