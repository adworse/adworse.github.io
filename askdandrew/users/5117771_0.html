Thread in How To Submit A Deal

Dear Corey, How do we know the bank is willing to satisfy the first mortgage for $5MM? Can/Will they turn-around and request a deficiency judgement for the unpaid $2.5MM? Are we supposed to negotiate with the first mortgage holder to get the offer to satisfy for $5MM? Sincerely, Paul

Paul Halstrom commented Jan 19 2014
  • staff

    Paul:

    Great question. Banks will let us know if they want to take a DPO (“Discounted Pay Off”) by sending a Term Sheet to us or the current borrower stating so.

    On that T/S (“Term Sheet”) they will also place on there if this is a release or a satisfaction of judgment.

    Remember, each deal is unique and no 2 deals will ever be exactly the same.

    Thanks,
    Sal

Thread in Marketing - Pearl

Dear Staff,

While Sal, Corey, and Pearl were commiserating over indications that only 2% of commercial real estate brokers know what we intermediaries do; I had to stop and ask myself if I know.

Fictitious Scenario:

Entity owns a commercial property with a balloon payment due in a year.

Entity has attempted to re-fi with the current lending bank.

Due to same lender having too many loans with this entity – all in the same asset class – the lender has offered a discounted pay off (DPO).

Entity seeks a commercial real estate broker to market, sell, and close on this property within one-year; to cover the DPO.

Property suffers from deferred maintenance, low occupancy rates, and high turnover of tenants.

As an intermediary, I go to the broker and ask if there are any deals they have which we may assist in.

The broker explains the above situation and we meet with the borrower. I make clear that – if the borrower can provide roughly 35% of the DPO as fresh equity – there’s a good chance that we can finance the remaining balance for a couple of years, provide additional funds to bring maintenance current, as well as funds for upgrades like painting, carpeting, and some appliances.

My hope, in doing this, is to enhance the tenants’ living space, reduce tenancy turnover, increase the occupancy rate, and lengthen the available time to sell the property. All of these matters add value, improve the ‘curb appeal’ to potential buyers, and help the broker sell the property.

This means the property may sell for more, which increases the commercial real estate broker’s commission. It also means that broker will be representing a ‘better’ property, which advances their own status amongst their peers and potential listings.

Is this what y’all meant? Am I in the 2%? Out of the ballpark? Somewhere in between?

Sincerely,

Paul Halstrom
