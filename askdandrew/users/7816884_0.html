    Nique

    First, that was a nice check. *$37,000 is not a bad payday.* Now you have the deal junkie virus in your blood. You’ll never recover!

    Great question. If you’re doing residential deals, you’re better off doing them on a deal-by-deal basis. Now, before you take me off of the Christmas card list, listen to what I have to tell you:

    *1. You’ll make more money – in the short term – by taking 5 points or whatever you’re charging.*
    Your investors will want to see you more in action. Give them checks. But more than that, make sure that everything you’re doing is buttoned down – meaning, place them into a servicing company who sends them automatic payments or checks. It is most important for you to show them how you are “managing” their money. Not just collecting a fee. Make sense? You’re building a brand here for yourself.

    *2. If, and only if, you can find enough deal flow, do a small raise around that.*
    This is where people get into trouble. They take in capital, but they feel pressured to deploy it. The worst reason to invest in anything or deploy capital in this case is because you feel like you have to.

    You can run a successful brokerage model without having to take money and lock it up.

    I think the reasons your investors are pushing back is that 2 years is a long time when you a) have a presidential election coming up, b) they want to be able to have discretion on what deals they go into and not rely solely on your discretion yet and c) the world is nervous as a result of Paris, etc.

    If you were to ask them after the next Presidential Election, then you may have a better chance of getting them to lock up with you. However, now, its going to be tough. Liquidity is key. And you must show that you have deal flow (see #2 above) to deploy it - *meaningfully*.

    Your Pal,
    Sal

    Nique: Great question.

    The trust deed is recorded first lien position. The owners of that lien are those who invested in that fractionalized trust deed (or mortgage in MD).

    The fractionalized mortgage investors are not in any different position. They are all first position, with different ownership interests. Like an airplane. its going to NYC, however, some people paid more than others (i.e.: first class). If it crashes, everyone dies.

    Makes sense?
