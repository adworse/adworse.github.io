  • Luan,
    Different people have different appetites so someone out there may find interest in your deal but when reviewing your information I have the following concerns and comments that will hopefully provide you with some value.
    1) Provide the meat to the deal upfront. Save the narrative or country music song after you've given your prospective funding sources something to analyze in a few minutes otherwise there not going to even bother. Dandrew tends to be a bit more gentle when it comes to this, but the rest of the capital markets will just delete your message and move on, eventually flagging you all together.
    2) Without getting into too much I'll stick to the main issue...cash flow.
    In the current market place those with capital are only concerned with one thing, income producing assets. So if you are going to put weight on any one data point more than anything else it should be income and more importantly net operating income. For example, you provided gross income 3 years ago which to be honest doesn’t mean much, especially when you consider the uncertainty of the actual business she is running (antiques) and the ongoing health concerns. You then provided the recent gross income of $100k, so at a 10 cap (which is generous) this deal, to a funding source, is only worth $1mm, but that’s gross, the net is obviously less and is the number that matters most, keeping in mind that debt service isn’t calculated in NOI so free and clear or not doesn’t matter, there is still some type of operating expenses, utilities, etc, debt service comes after, which would also kill this deal. It might be worth more to a prospective buyer who sees a more lucrative use for the property, however an offer doesn’t dictate value and is not the same as an appraisal, which by the way is also, in many cases, meaningless to a funding source. I could “write” you an offer tomorrow for $10mm, but that doesn’t constitute real market value, unless I actually close, but if there aren’t similar sales at that price level in the area it would likely be considered a one-off sale and not seen as a useable comp.
    So without getting into my additional concerns such as small square footage, mom& pop standalone retail, and so on, I don’t think this is a deal for the significant fact that it doesn’t have the cash flow or financials to merit funding. The only thing that comes to mind is cross-collateral. If the sponsor has other real estate owned or assets that would help hedge the risk of a $2mm loan then maybe you can keep this one alive, but I wouldn’t fall in love with this deal, or any deal for that matter, especially not those short on cash flow. Stick to the numbers in the beginning and make a quick subjective analysis and proceed as necessary from there.
    However, like I said, that’s just my opinion and how I look at it and in no way means that you couldn’t find a loan based on some other criteria, but I don’t think this is something that is worth a considerable amount of time. Good luck!

  • Luan. Thank you for your questions. Dandrew would not look to fund this deal.
    1) We only consider retail deals that are grocery or pharmacy anchored (the thinking being that we all need to eat and get prescriptions/vitamins etc).
    2) As Jon points out the estimated value of the asset is well below the loan request.
    3) Current NOI would not be sufficient to support the debt.
    4) The lawsuit is against the company and she is looking to leverage her asset to pay the company's debts. In essence, a cashout refi, whereby the money is going somewhere else and not to the asset (which is what we would look for).
    5) We do not like the scenario whereby the owner is the sole tenant of the building. The income of the business is directly proportional to the health of the owner. Would you/your company look to lend this person money knowing that her ability to make repayments depends on her health and ability to sell antiques/interior design work? With the lawsuit and pending foreclosure, this can only add to the stress...
    6) Cross-collateral - our business is Real Estate and would recommend sticking to this for cross-collateral as we can value it.
    7) Being able to rent out the remainder of the building seems to be a useful and welcome boost to overall cashflow no? Of course, this depends on the liklihood of her losing the property and how much time she has to complete this (not everyone wishes to work at boosting occupancy if likely to lose the property). If she has been focusing on the business and not had time to do this then she should certainly consider using a professional.
    I hope that this helps to outline the thinking behind our standpoint.
