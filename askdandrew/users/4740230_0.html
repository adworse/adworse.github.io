First thing I do is I do exactly what Sal tells us to do, use The FDIC infographic and the steps listed in their exact order. I don't deviate from it!!!

Tip... Do not reinvent the wheel, the things Sal teaches are not trial and error, they work but only if you do it the right way.

Next thing I do is I print off the entire information for each bank I find. I want to have that in front if me when I call. I then segment the banks by city and I begin to setup days I'm calling. I devote one day to each city or area.

I put each bank in order by the amount of loans and reo's they have, going highest to lowest. The higher the volume, the more in trouble they are in and more likely to talk to me.

When I call, I introduce myself and my company. I always ask for the CFO, Chief Credit Officer or Director of Whole Loan Trading.

Once I get them on the phone, I re-introduce myself and simply tell them the following:

"We understand that they have a significant amount of loans and oreo's that have been reported as being in a state of less than performing to some level and I would like to know how much they would need to dispose of to get their balance sheets back to a level that will put them in a position of being more healthy".

I'm wording it in a way that they can feel like I'm genuinely trying to be a problem solver. The next thing I say to them is that we are looking for first lien position loans, performing, re-performing, scratch and dent or NPN.

It's at this point that I inform them that the volume level that they may want to dispose of may be more than we can afford, however, I am backed by a fund that is financially strong and are also providers of solutions and ask if I can bring them into the deal.

I'm being up front, I'm letting them know that I on my own May not be able to buy but the fund I work with, is in a position.

Then I cut them off at the what will you pay pass by informing them that bids are made as principal bids and are based upon market conditions. I also tell them if your looking for a bid right now, today without us or the fund having the benefit of the proper initial review, we will not be a player in your space.

I then ask when can I expect a tape from you and I tell them what we need to see in order to be in a position to have a successful initial bid.

I wanted to pass on something to the members that you have went out of the way to reinforce in getting my thinking to come full circle on the subject of risk.

Here goes. It's so easy to want a deal to be real, to put the cart before the horse, I do it, it's something that I'm working on. Sal has told me just because something looks like a deal, doesn't make it so.

Pragmatically speaking, a deal really isn't a deal until the deal is scrutinized, has gone through due diligence and terms are created and agreed to and then you can say it's a deal. Until that time, it's an opportunity that needs to prove out.

You have to look at the risks. This is prudent business. It's so easy to look at the financial rewards first because you want to get paid.

Everyone wants the big score, the huge fees, etc. This is why the players like Blackstone for example focus on the larger deals. Why they undertake capital raises to create a two billion dollar fund. They are building a financial war chest so they can buy those $250 million dollar deals and up, or the myriad of ninety million dollar resort deals, etc.

It's easy to bypass risk because as an Intermediary, you have no risk in a transaction because your not writing the check. Your risk is the time your investing at the front end and the only thing you risk is losing a payday and maybe a equity piece on the back end. The funder risks losing their capital, their credibility, etc. People never remember the great deals you do, they remember the bad deals.

You have to ask yourself what can possibly go wrong if my capital provider gets into this deal and it goes south at any point in time during the term of the deal. On a funding deal, what can go wrong at 12 months, 18 months, 24 months, the day before the term ends. On a syndicated deal, what happens if the sponsor pulls the trigger on a bad decision, are the investors going to have to do a capital call and put more equity in, just to make the deal whole, etc.

For a fund such as Dandrew, it's more than just capital. It's reputations, credibility, future abilities to gain the trust of investors to place monies again, investors monies, pension funds monies. This is why Sal and Dandrew just do not buy every deal. It's more than just deploying capital. Money has two voices and two tolerance levels, pleasure and pain.
