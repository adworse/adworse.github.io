Many of the formulas we rely on include a "loan constant." What is the easiest and most accurate way for us to grab the loan constant for a given project?

Dave Dejewski commented Mar 26 2015
  • staff

    I think you figured this out. Can you explain your answer here?

    Sal

    Salvatore Buscemi commented Mar 27 2015

  • I did figure this out. The loan constant (K) is the true rate of interest payment made. It takes into account the loan spread and amortization. In other words:

    - a loan that is interest only will have a "true" interest rate equal to the constant. The payment is equal to the interest.
    - a loan that is amortized is changing (principal reduction over time) as a result of that amortization. The actual rate of interests payment is changing (the rate at which interest is paid is higher) along with it.

    We have to figure out what the real interest rate is as amortization slowly changes the balance of principal and interest as percentages of a payment over time.

    The loan constant (K) something we can calculate on our own.

    The formula is as follows: =PMT(Interest Rate/12, Amortization*12,-1)*12

    Where:
    PMT = the payment
    Interest rate = the annual interest rate
    Amortization = the number of years the loan is to be amortized
    We use 12 because there are 12 months in a year.

    For example, a loan with a published 8% interest rate would have a loan constant (K) (calculated by Excel) of:
    =PMT(.08/12,30*12,-1)*12 - or - 8.81%

    If this loan were amortized over 15 years instead of 30, the loan constant would be 11.47%
    If this were amortized over 3 years instead of 30, the loan constant would be 37.6% - meaning the borrower would be paying back a little more than 1/3 of the interest back in that 3 year period.
    Amortized over 1 year, the loan constant would be 104.39%
    If it were dragged out to 50 years, it would be 8.15%

    K will always be higher on an amortized loan than it will be on an interest-only loan.
